Q Ratings for Zines

Mark Nelson

(Reprinted from _Everything_ 92, June 1995. )

In Everything 90 (October 1994) I introduced the concept of a Q rating for zines. The Q rating is an attempt to find the zine which runs, or rans in the case of folded zines, the `best' games. There are many ways in which `best' may be defined. Considerations such as game-fee, cost of zine, frequency and reliability of zine and quality of GMing are important factors which can be quantified. Considerations such as non-game content (letter column, articles, games other than diplomacy on offer) are personal considerations which can't be quantified. The Q rating is an attempt to find the `best' zine by calculating a number.

One way to quantify which zines offer `good' games of diplomacy is to examine their dropout rate. Zines with low dropout rates are more likely to contain enthusiastic players. You may be less likely to win if you play in these zines (is this true?!) but you are more likely to enjoy the game (unless, of course, winning is the sole criteria for judging your enjoyment of the game!).

If the average length of a game in two zines differs then comparing the average number of dropouts would be meaningless because the longer a game lasts the more dropouts you expect. Hence zines which have longer games, which would also seem to be a good indication of quality, might have a higher dropout rate than zines with shorter games. Instead of quantifying zines by their average drop-out we quantity them by their average drop-out rate per game-year played (Q). This is defined as the ratio of average game-length to average drop-out rate and has non-SI units of years per drop-out (!).

Hence to calculate the Q-ranking the average number of dropouts and the average game-length are required for each zine in the survey. There were 181 non-orphaned regular diplomacy games reported in Everything 85 (May 1992) through Everything 91 (March 1995). These 181 completed games were played in 45 zines. For the North American Hobby we have

Q(Postal) = 10.42/2.75 = 3.80 years per drop-out.

Table One contains contains statistical information on the 15 zines which ran five, or more, games to completion and their Q rankings. These zines account for 126 (69.6%) of the games reported and can be reasonable considered as the `backbone' of the postal diplomacy hobby throughout this period. note that of the 15 zines, 8 have a Q ranking lower than the hobby average.

Although zines which have a reputation as places to play good games of diplomacy also have high Q-values too much emphasis should not be placed on the ranking of zines by Q-value; the sample size is small and the standard deviation in the number of dropouts and gamelength is large. A GM that has a solid core of players that play in a couple of games with a torpid turnaround could show up very highly in this survey, this would not necessarily be a place to play. Let us mearly note that Boast and Graustark appear to the `best' zines to play in, followed closely by CCC, Cheesecake and Hoodwink and then Maniac's Paradise.

Remember --- The Q ranking is a guide, it does not provide the definitive answer to finding the zine which runs the `best' games in the hobby!
