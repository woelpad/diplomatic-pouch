INTRODUCTION

    Welcome to America and welcome to WDC X!

    This collection of bon mots is designed to both entertain and stimulate you. Who knows, perhaps you will even find it useful. To that end I've included a lot of URLs and even a few links to critical web sites.   I plan to discuss this year's WDC event, the host venues of Baltimore and Washington, diplomacy & diplomats in the real world of Washington's Foggy Bottom, next year's WDC XI in Paris, and the most talked about WDC XII host candidate for 2002, Canberra.  Finally, since we'll already be in asia, I'll offer a few diplocentric thoughts on my recent asian tour.

    My theme is "In search of the perfect..." and I invite you to come along as I search for, in no particular order: the perfect national anthem; the perfect chocolate;  the perfect hamburger; the perfect museum and memorial; the perfect sleeping partner; the perfect American holiday;  the perfect WDC event; the perfect Diplomacy web site; the perfect school for Dippers; and the Dipper of the decade.  It should be an interesting hunt, don't you think?

WDC X

    If you ask the average American, or foreigner for that matter, to name the United States of America national anthem you'll probably get any one of a half-dozen titles, depending on how old the respondent is.  You might get  "The Star-Spangled Banner,"  "The Battle Hymn of the Republic," "The Stars and Stripes Forever," "America, the Beautiful," "God Bless America," or even "This Land Is Your Land;" Woody Guthries' response to Irving Berlin's tune!  And, in a way, they are all America's national anthems, as you'll discover if you spend much time in Baltimore or Washington this summer. When you come to WDC X you'll be in America's patriotic heartland!  Enjoy it.

    This year's WDC X is being hosted by the BPA, a local gaming organization, that took over the old AVALONCON, formerly hosted by Avalon Hill, now a part of Hasbro, the current manufacturers of Diplomacy, and just about every other boardgame sold in America.

     A lot has changed since I attended my first AVALONCON some eight or so years ago, but much is still the same.  The Freemason Home is still up on the hill across the way, but I hope now they won't try to shoot you if you drive on to their property; which is what they tired to do to me when I last visited.  McCormick/Schilling is now the world's largest spice company (and they'll be even bigger when they complete their purchase of France's largest spice company), but no longer the owner of the venue hotel. How else do you think it got a restaurant named The Cinnamon Tree?  There's still an ice cream parlor and Pizza Hut in the hotel, but I don't know if we'll be able to enjoy the smell of garlic throughout the event.  Avalon Hill and the Dotts, Tom Shaw, and Rex Martin are gone; but Don Greenwood is still running the show.  The shopping mall down the street is still there, but probably doesn't have the highest vacancy rate in the country as it did back then.  The local bank, Capitol One, has become a big-time player; mostly on the basis of a high-risk credit card portfolio.  Hopefully the wine merchant down the road is now carrying more than E&J Gallo.  Robert Sacks, Diplomacy's senior citizen enfante terrible is still around; but our favorite game, Fast Food Franchise, isn't on the list of events this year.  Finally, to everyone's joy I'm sure, the Gettysburg observation tower went up with a bang on July 3rd!

    There have been three prior WDCs held in the United States: II (1990) in Chapel Hill, North Carolina; where we all got an exposure to classic southern hospitality; VI (1996) in Columbus, Ohio; where we all saw the strength and wealth of the American midwest; and VIII (1998), again in Chapel Hill; where we got a taste of Carolina BBQ.  To learn more about these events, and the rest of WDC's history, visit The WDC Shrine .

    And now, for our first WDC event of the new millennium, we come to Hunt Valley, Maryland; which allows us to claim both Baltimore and Washington as our host cities.  Together these two cities represent America's deepest roots and traditions.  I hope you'll take the time to visit both cities and their surrounding areas.  Whether you only have a few days or a whole month; you'll find an amazing variety of things to see and do.

    But first, the WDC event.  What do our hosts have planned for us?  Most of the details can be found on the BPA or WDCX website, or found in the BPA program, but I'll share a few highlights with you here.  The events will be held in the Garden Room on the hotel's main floor (upwind from the Pizza Hut, I hope) and will run from Thursday through Sunday.
Thursday is pretty much an open day for people to do what they want.  There's been talk of a Colonial Dip event, a meeting of con movers and doers, some pre-Charter lobbying, etc.  It's pretty much up to you.  The solo Dip tournament will be in four rounds, played on Friday (at 0900 and 1800), Saturday (at 0800), and Sunday (at 0800). There will be an Escalation Dip event going on, a team event concurrent with the Saturday round, a talk by Allan Calhamer on Saturday afternoon, followed by the WDC Society meeting, and a blind gunboat tournament Saturday night at 1900.  The results and awards will come sometime Sunday afternoon.  There will also be an auction of goodies sometime during the event to raise funds to help defray expenses.  I expect Jim Yerkey to do his usual high-style, low-profile job of running these events.

    The details of the gaming I leave to those interested.  The WDC Society meeting, on Saturday afternoon, is a must for those interested in the game hobby.  It's also usually the most entertaining event of each WDC.  This year's agenda is full of interesting goodies for attendees to pick over.  First up is the adoption of the draft Charter that Edi Birsan and his cohorts have been working on since the beginning of time.  Almost as important is the question of incorporating the WDC.
Those present will also pick a site for the 2001 North American DipCon (Denver or ?), affirm the 2001 WDC event in Paris, and select a 2002 WDC site (Canberra or ?).  Edi has invested a huge amount of time and effort into orchestrating this year's meeting.  It will be interesting to see if everyone can and will keep in step to the rhythm of his drum.

    If, and it is always a big if, everyone who said they are coming shows up; and if everyone works together in a positive way, as they have said they will; this should be the most successful WDC held in North America.  If not...

    Although I've not seen any suggestions thus far from the organizers, I assume we'll have somekind of post-event feed. My suggested location for that is Alonso's, just down the freeway from the Con site. I'll explain why later.
 

BALTIMORE

    Baltimore isn't on anybody's short list of "must see" places in America.  Maybe that's good. Maybe it's bad.  You
decide.  Maryland has been offered as a sort of "miniature America," with something for everybody and a good sampling of  what America has to offer.  Baltimore prides itself on its "charm," but it also has a very high murder and syphilis rate.
Whether there's a connection between those three; I don't know.  Baltimore isn't a world class city, or even a white collar one.  It's definitely blue collar, and its strong point is its culturally diverse neighborhoods. There are more than 80 of them, ranging from a few square blocks to entire planned communities with thousands of residents.   I'll be suggesting some places for you to visit, and things to do, but keep in mind that Baltimore's real charm lies in its people.

    There are over 40 historical sites in greater Baltimore (registered  as such by the US National Park Service).  Here are some sites you should check out if you can: Fort McHenry National Monument and Historic Shrine (required for all Brits), Captain Larry's (offers the best hamburger in town), the Baltimore National Aquarium (in case you wanna see what a real piranha, stingray, vine snake, poison frog, anaconda, crocodile, or shark looks like) & Inner Harbor (touristy, but nice, Washington Monument (one of the loveliest squares in America) & Walters Museum (fantastic collection of asian goodies), Alonso's (best cheeseburger in town), Hampton National Historic Site (see how the American aristocracy lived when the French were demolishing the Bastille), and the McCormick spice operation here in Hunt Valley (in case you don't know whether marijuana and heroin are herbs or spices).  If you're into baseball there's also the Camden Yards stadium and Babe Ruth Museum. If you're into Black history there's a lot sites related to famous blacks from Baltimore and jazz. If you're a literary snob you can check the home turfs of Edgar Allen Poe, John Dos Passos or Tom Clancy! And last, but not least, there's the B&O railroad museum for choo-choo fans!  As I said, there's a lot to do in Baltimore. I guarantee you'll run out of time and/or money before you run out of things to do.

    If you have time (all of these require a day trip away from Baltimore) be sure to visit some of the Civil War battlefields surrounding Baltimore.  Antietam and Gettysburg are both very impressive, but get there early or late to avoid the tourist hordes. If you're alone and it's quiet Gettysburg, in particular, will effect you the way Verdun or Passachendale will.  And, if you have another day, drive out to the Eastern Shore and see the peninsula that Delaware, Maryland and Virginia share, along with millions of blue crabs, chickens, and Tom Clancy fans!   If you've got a fast car, see how many states you can visit in one day!  If you're good you should be able to pick up at least a half-dozen.

    All of these sites I've mentioned have web sites, so check those out; or ask me about them at the Con.  I'll have maps and guidebooks.

For general information on Baltimore check out: the Baltimore Sun.
 

WASHINGTON

    If this is your first visit to Washington, DC, you need at least a month to really get to know it.  Any less and you're only scratching the surface.  So, what can you do in two days?   Actually quite a lot, if you plan ahead.

    I was fortunate to be able to spend seven weeks in Washington back in the days when another Californian occupied the White House (Nixon, not Reagan).  At that time I did just about everything a tourist is supposed to do in America's capital.  Since then; I've found that an occasional two or three day trip every ten years or so is enought to keep up with the changes in the city.  This visit I plan to only spend two days.  Hopefully that will be enough, but there's a lot of new stuff to see.

    But  if you're visiting from overseas or for the first time there's a wealth of treasures to be discovered in DC.  Brits and Frenchmen would probably argue that the museums of London and Paris (80 plus in number, for each) contain more treasures than Washington, D.C. Rome, Madrid, Vienna, or even St.Petersburg might say the same thing, but I would disagree.  The British and French museums represent the loot of their empires.  Rome represents the wealth of the church. Madrid's collection is great, but spotty. The Vienna collections represents the tastes of the Habsburgs, and the treasures of St.Petersburg the tastes of the Romanovs (far superior, but still incomplete).  The museums of Washington, on the other hand, represent the best that money could buy, or the most valuable of gifts imaginable.  The collections scattered along The Mall represent the greatest collection of art in the world.  And that's only the half of it.  The other half of The Mall represents the collective memories of the world's second great empire.  To see more, you would have to go to Rome's Forum.

    Anyway, Washington, unlike Baltimore, is a city of government, of paper, and of white collar bureaucrats.  There are no real industries, except perhaps for the lobbyists and tourists.  It's a city of whites (15% of the population) vs. blacks (85%), but that isn't as bad as it sounds.  It's true that the number of blacks and other minorities in the higher levels of government is low, but it's also a fact that there are tens of thousands of dual income black families in Washington making a good living.  Blacks may make up a a big majority of the capitol's janitors, security guards, and maids; but they also make up 80% of the city's Cadillac buyers!  So, it's a complex city of a few thousand politicians supported by ten thousand or so professionals, a hundred thousand or so support staff, and the traditional civic support workers.  And yes, it's true, you will see more California plates in DC than you will DC plates.

    Old-time Dippers may recall that one of the seven players in the first ever (1964A) postal Diplomacy game was named Washington Perry, a black senior civil servant from Washington, DC.  I plan to try and track him down, if he's still alive,  while I'm in town.

    Washington in June of 1972 was far different than Washington in August of 2000.  Then, you automatically arranged your visit around trips to the Capitol, White House, Supreme Court, and the big three Memorials (Washington, Lincoln and Jefferson).  Today, all that is changed.  You can do Washington without visiting any of those places, but you'd be a fool.
In addition, you should try to see The Vietnam Veterans Memorial, the National Air and Space Museum, the National Gallery of Art, the National Archives' Charters of Freedom, and Mt. Vernon.

    There are now nearly 80 museums, memorials and other sites to visit in Washington.  Almost as many as London or Paris.  The big difference, of course, is that almost all of them, including the best ones, are FREE!  The National Park Service and Smithsonian operate most of the best sites, and rarely charge admission.   Check out these two web sites:

National Park Service  and the Smithsonian

In addition the local newspaper site has a lot of good info on what's happening in DC:

The Washington Post

    Here's a list of things you "might do" during your visit: afternoon tea at the top of Washington National Cathedral, visiting the Lincoln Memorial after dark, taking a monument and memorial walking tour, visit Rock Creek Park, spend a day in Alexandria, visit the Supreme Court, spend a morning on the Mall, visit Union Station, stroll down Embassy Row and see how many black dots you can pick up, people-watch at Dupont Circle, take a Potomac cruise, visit the Georgetown Flea Market, shop at the Eastern Market, have a drink on the Rooftop Terrace of the Hotel Washington.  There are also various tram bus and self-guided walking tours of the Mall area available.  Use them, and the Metro!  Parking in DC is scarce and expensive!

    For this visit I plan to visit a few new sites and some one-of-a-kind shows.  I'll give you an idea of what I'll be doing in DC the Monday and Tuesday after the Con, just in case you're interested.

    Celebrating the 1,000th anniversary of the arrival of the Vikings in North America at the National Museum of Natural History.

    Viewiing images (wood block prints) from the Russo-Japanese Front (1904-05) at the Sackler Gallery.

    Touring the Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts.

    Seeing the works of America's greatest native artist, Norman Rockwell, at the Corcoran Gallery of Art.

    Making headlines at one of Washington's most popular new museums, the NEWSEUM. It's over in Arlington, but well worth the trip.

    Sampling America's national food dish, the hamburger, at Five Guys, Clyde's, Old Ebbitt Grill, Chadwick's, Johnny Rockets, Ireland's Four Provinces, or the Cafe Deluxe.

    Checking for bugs and leaks at The Watergate Hotel; which is where I'll be staying, but not in room 702!

DIPLOMACY & DIPLOMATS

    Washington is the center of American diplomacy, of course.  As such it should be of interest to any student of Diplomacy.  There are a lot of places you can visit and things you can do if you're interested in diplomacy.  Here's a few examples:
    Check out this site: Digital Diplomacy for Students
    As you check it out, keep in mind that our State Dept didn't even have a web site before Madeleine Albright was Secretary of State.
    Several schools in the Washington area offer fine programs for wouldbe professional Dippers:
    The semi-official inhouse training program for the State Dept
    The Paul H. Nitze School of Advanced International Studies
    A bit further afield (in Boston) is Tufts University's School of Law and Diplomacy; which recently proved that even the best can screw up big time when they announced a new dean had been appointed, BEFORE he resigned as our ambassador to South Korea, or we told the Koreans he was leaving.
    And our very own Diplomacy institution, The Institute for Diplomatic Studies, has been successfully cloned by The Egyptian Ministry of Foreign Affairs.

    The eighth floor our Department of State Building, in Foggy Bottom, is devoted to the offices of the Secretary of State and the Diplomatic Reception Rooms of the Department of State. Check out that web site if you're interested in visiting them.  And, while you're there, stop by and present your credentials to Madeleine Albright, who is wrapping up a career as one of America's most unique secretaries of state.  I've followed her career with interest and admiration for many years, and a few of you may remember my presentation on her in Columbus.  Since then she has continued to impress and amaze me, although she too has had her occasional screw up (e.g. the Okinawa school meeting hall name fiasco).

WDC XI

    WDC XI is supposed to be in Paris next February.  I write "supposed to be" because nobody really knows what, if anything, the French are planning for the event; and that includes the French themselves.  Hopefully we will find out more in Baltimore.

    Today is Bastille Day and I'm reviewing some articles I've gathered from the media about France and the French, and that includes the French media. None of them are much different than what we've read before.  Still, there are new twists and turns in the on-going soap opera that is France Today!

    French soccer rules!  World champions and European champions!  Yes!!!  But where are the French players on the French team?

    The Tour de France is well under way, but where are the French leaders?

    Paris is on holiday, if their picnic wasn't rained out. Snow in summer?  Sacre bleu!

    The French president has proposed letting the people vote on whether his term of office should be changed from two seven year terms to two five year terms; thereby hoping to guarantee himself a second term in office.

    The French are complaining that the US is spying on them, even though they have their own, similar system.
It's a case of Echelon vs. Frenchelon!

    The French are complaining that America's McDonald's is destroying their eating habits, even though the real issue is the huge subsidies the EU and France continues to hand out to its own farmers.

    Airbus is complaining about Boeing's favored status in America. Strange, nobody in France complained when Vivendi bought out Seagram's!

    The French are in an uproar because a McDonald's employee was fired for feeding the poor, but nobody has complained about Alain Ducasse charging $400 per person to feed the rich in New York!

    The French are up in arms because the Languedoc wine region; which has always produced the poorest wines in France; has been invaded by Robert Mondavi, an American winemaker.  I don't recall the French complaining when their winemakers bought up large pieces of prime vineyards in America, Chile, etc.

    And what do the French say?  They worry that applications to the ENA are down 30% in the last four years alone.

    They celebrate, today, the storming of the Bastille, even as their own media reveals that French prisons today are no better now than they were then.

    Still, as the 23 million visitors visitors to Paris this year will affirm, WE LOVE PARIS!  So, prepare yourselves, my French Diplomates!  WE ARE COMING!

WDC XII

    Rumor has it that the Ozzies (Australians) and Kiwis (New Zealanders) are bidding to host WDC XII in 2002.  Edi Birsan has already visited the Ozzies and given them his seal of approval, so we can all assume it's a done deal.  If you have any doubts yourself you can visit the 2000 New Zealand Diplomacy Championship this coming October in Auckland.  If you run into me, not to worry.  I'm just there to check on the state of the America's Cup!

    A few, very few, of you may remember that WDC III was actually held in Canberra, Australia in 1992.  Basically the new bid is a rerun of that event, with a few new faces tossed in.  I remember the discussion in Birmingham in 1988 about WDC rotations, and the further discussions in Chapel Hill in 1990.  The two big points the Ozzies made then were: 1) hosting a WDC would be shot in the arm to the Diplomacy hobby "down-under," and 2) lots of foreigners would come because it was a cheap place to visit.  Well, neither happened.  After the event, the local hobbies crashed; and only a handful of foreigners could afford the trip.

    And, it appears from here, the Kiwis at least, are suffering from The French Diease.  Consider the case of Charlie Dempsey, FIFA, and the World Cup bidding; the US sale of F-16s to New Zealand, their reception of our ambassador Carol Moseley-Braun, and, worst of all, the defection of three key  NZ America's Cup team members to an Italian financed, Swiss labelled syndicate.  Shame, Shame!

    Still, if we can go to Paris next year after what happened during their 1995 event, I don't see why we can't go back to Canberra.  Just make sure Brandon Clarke is running things!  :-)  He's got more energy and faster fingers than Edi does.

    And don't worry about a place to stay, I've already booked The Diplomat Boutique Hotel!  Canberra should be an interesting place to see, and you can combine that with Sydney (if you're into self-proclaimed world class cities) or such civilized places as Melbourne, Adelaide, or Perth. However, based on my culinary experiences in Goteborg, stay away from the ostrich, 'roo, and kiwi steaks.  They're tough, just like their Dippers.
