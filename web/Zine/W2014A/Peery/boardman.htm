THE BOARDMAN NUMBER CUSTODIAN:
FIFTY YEARS AND STILL GOING, BUT JUST BARELY

by Tom Howell and Larry Peery

When I first contemplated writing this article I was sad because I knew the number of new postal Diplomacy game starts last year had dropped to one, 2014A. That fact made me realize two things: 1) The postal Diplomacy hobby as I have known it for some fifty years was, for all practical purposes, nearly dead; and 2) that meant the hobby’s oldest custodial position, the Boardman Number Custodian (BNC), was almost obsolete. I also realized that I didn’t even know who the current BNC was, assuming there was one. And it dawned on me that the number of people active in the hobby who were still playing postal Diplomacy games was shrinking fast; and that by far most Diplomacy players today have never experienced postal Diplomacy as I knew it and, indeed, many of them probably don’t even know that there was a postal Diplomacy hobby before the advent of computers, the internet, and the computerized game adjudication systems commonly called judge.

And so with the help of a few hobby Old Farts I tracked down the current BNC, Tom Howell. Tom obviously is still doing what the BNC was established to do --- keeping track of the hobby’s statistical records (e.g. game starts, game names (if any), game number assignments, game masters, publications, players of record, and game results). It’s just that today there’s not much for him to do.

Tom provided almost all the information in this article and for that I thank him. Without it this story wouldn’t have been written and it could have happened that the BNC would have faded away into obscurity like so many other hobby institutions; and that would have been a shame. However, after some reflection I realize this should not be an occasion for sadness but rather an appreciation for the change that has taken place in the hobby. It is that change from postal to electronic mail that has kept the hobby not only going but expanding and even enjoying a mini-boom of sorts. So, let’s look back not with anger or regret, but with pride and happiness.

What follows are several emails I exchanged with Tom that basically tell the story of the BNC in the last few years, some history and a bit of Tom’s story as well. I decided not to delete some of the personal comments in our emails that deal with health issues, etc. because I think these help show that the BNC was and is not only a statistician but also a human being and that we are, like the BNC position, getting older and more frail as the years go by.

Another purpose in publishing this in this form is to provide the future custodians of the PBEM hobby with a model for how they can do what people like Tom and I have been doing for the last fifty years. For instance, Tom alludes to the fact that he doesn’t know much about the PBEM hobby or have much contact with the international hobby. That’s a shame but it’s also a concern. It raised the question in my mind, is anybody keeping track on a worldwide hobby basis of PBEM Diplomacy game records? Additionally, are the various national hobbies keeping track of their own records. This particularly concerns me about the revitalized or new hobbies in places like South Africa or China? I know from past experience how difficult it is to reconstruct the early years of a hobby, so I hope these hobbies will learn from our mistakes.

Tom Howell replied in his second email:

Personally, I tend to discount what doctors tell me. I have a-fib, and as far as I can tell, the only thing that triggers it is my getting dehydrated. Acute dehydration will almost always start up a-fib, and I don't recall an episode when I wasn't dehydrated. Dehydration is something that has always been a problem, but now I seriously have to watch my hydration level. If I haven't stayed hydrated and start into an episode, first I force water down, then I go for a long walk. In general, I don't get enough exercise, but when I am exercising, it definitely seems to help the heart stay on schedule.

If your doctor didn't tell you, there is a tendency for folks to have more frequent episodes of a-fib as they go along. If that's the case, it can become so bad that it becomes very difficult to get the heart back to a normal pace. You probably know how to take your own pulse. I would suggest you start taking it regularly, several times a day - or more frequently. As you monitor your pulse, you may find that you can tell, without your finger on an artery, generally when you are quiet, that you can sense the general speed of your heart beat. This is a good thing. If you find it very fast when you aren't exercising, you're in a-fib. If this happens, try to correlate it with something that may have caused it. You may have causes different from mine. When you figure out the likely triggers, then the thing to do is whatever will remove those conditions. Hope this helps.

Peery Notes: I’ve been lucky enough to know all of these people and worked with many over the years. I was always amazed at how hard they worked on their often thankless task, especially in the Golden Days of Postal Diplomacy when there were scores or even hundreds of game starts per year. I remember talking about this to Kathy Byrne Caruso. She told me she hated being BNC (She was a bookkeeper by profession.) because it was too much like work, but she did it because of her love for the game and hobby. I think that pretty much sums up every BNC’s innermost thoughts. I wonder what Tom thinks? Special mention should be made of Conrad von Mezke’s seven years as BNC and for having had the courage to tackle the job twice!

As for your long shot: Chris Babcock and I have been sporadically working on that. I don't have time to work on it currently, but hope to get back to it after the house is finished. The biggest problem is data entry. I have a bare bones website built in python, all the games from the BNC's journals are indexed, but only a small fraction of them have their game results entered. I got through about the mid-70 before other priorities pushed the project aside. The web site, if on-line, would allow anyone to enter results for games which are known to be complete, but do not have their results on-line.

Don't want to do much writing, but I'll provide any information that I have. Just ask: if I don't know I'll say so and try to send you to someone who may know.

Peery Notes: The hobby literature, particularly Diplomacy World in its early years, is full of information by and about the BNC and his or her work. Many of the BNCs published their own ‘zine devoted to their work and those can be found in Doug Kent’s Archives.

And there you have it, an update on one of the hobby’s most important institutions. The question is, who is going to step up to the plate and take on that task for the PBEM hobby?
