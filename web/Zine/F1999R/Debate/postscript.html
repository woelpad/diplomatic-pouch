Proposal 2 Postscript: The Science of Paradoxes

Disclaimer: this afterthought will probably only appeal to some physicists and sci-fi geeks, but what the heck.

I once heard or read an interesting theory from a physicist having to do with time travel and paradoxes. I don't exactly remember what the source was, but if it was something I read it was most likely in something like Scientific American, and if I heard it somewhere it was most likely at a lecture I heard by Lawrence Krauss, the physicist who wrote the book The Physics of Star Trek among others). Despite my incomplete recollection, I'm pretty sure the source of what I'm about to discuss was a "real" physicist. The discussion went something along the following lines:

We know that at the level of sub-atomic particles and quantum physics, time travel may be possible. In some situations, you can even view certain particles moving forward in time as those particles' antiparticles moving backwards in time, and this view is not inconsistent with the laws of physics as we understand them. As of now, however, we do not know how to make use of this to send information or matter back in time. But there are physicists who believe that it may be possible to someday have time travel. Perhaps not sending matter back in time, but perhaps information.

The classic time travel paradox situation says that if time travel were achieved by people, a time traveler could go back in time, kill one of his (or her) parents, preventing the traveler from ever being born, so that the traveler could never go back in time to kill the parent, so that there is nothing preventing the traveler from being born, so that the traveler would be born, and could go back in time and kill the parent, etc. Now this paradox would require sending matter back in time, but one could come up with a contrived scenario in which sending some signal or information back in time could result in somebody else's death, this paradox is in principle one that could occur even if time travel were restricted to information or a signal. Yes, that's a big if, in and of itself.

Now for the theory that was suggested in the lecture/article I mentioned. The universe is a complex mechanism. Such a paradox would throw this mechanism into some incomprehensible looping state that could not truly exist given the laws of physics. And therefore, there is something about the laws of physics that would prevent a paradox from ever arising, because a paradox is something that simply cannot physically exist in reality. It would be physically impossible for a time traveler to go back in time and kill his (or her) parent because it would lead to a situation that cannot physically exist.

I don't believe there is any physical evidence to support this theory, nor at the moment, even an experiment designed that could test the theory. It's more of a thought experiment on the part of some physicist or some group of physicists to answer the question "If time travel is indeed theoretically possible, what will stop the universe from being caught or destroyed by a paradox?" Put a little bit differently, some people have argued that time travel is impossible because it would lead to paradoxes, and physicists who believe that time travel may be possible are saying that (1) the possibility of a paradox caused by time travel does not necessarily make time travel impossible, and (2) if time travel is possible, paradoxes may not be a concern because it may be that paradoxes cannot exist.

Back to Diplomacy... in the case for proposal 2, I acknowledged that there was no real-life justification for why the army required to hold in rule 2 should have to hold. But maybe this isn't completely true. Loosely translated, one could restate the spirit of proposal 2 to something like: "If an army is ordered to move in a way that would cause a paradoxical situation, then that army is not allowed to move." Basically, this says that the army isn't moving because its moving would cause a paradox and a paradox cannot exist. And maybe, this does reflect a real-life situation because maybe there is something about the universe that will act to prevent a paradox because paradoxes are inherently forbidden by the physical laws of the universe.

Not very compelling, is it. I'm not particularly convinced by this argument myself. It sounds like somebody saying a paradoxical situation cannot exist because paradoxes cannot exist. If you assume one, then the other follows, but there's something a bit circular about it. And is there really a sound basis for thinking that a paradox cannot phsyically exist even if time travel is possible? People used to say that singularities could not exist physically and were merely a fabrication of mathematics, but I think it is commonly agreed now that black holes are indeed singularities in space-time. But for anyone who has nothing better to do than to read this far through my ruminations, there's some food for thought. And who knows... at some point in the distant future, some physicist may prove that there really is a real-life basis for proposal 2. Me, I think it's the best solution even if it's completely without foundation.
